Kaggle – RNA Folding project – 2025

Predicting RNA chemical reactivity profiles with deep learning
Kaggle competition “RNA Folding” · Team project (PyTorch)

As part of a team project, we participated in the Kaggle “RNA Folding” competition, which aims to predict base-wise chemical reactivity profiles (DMS / 2A3) from RNA sequences. A key difficulty of the challenge is that the training RNAs are relatively short, while the test set contains much longer molecules, so models must generalise to sequence lengths they have never seen.

We implemented and compared four architectures in PyTorch: a simple LSTM baseline, a BiLSTM, an LSTM with dilated Conv1D layers, and a Transformer. The Transformer achieved the best masked MAE on validation and test sets (0.28 / 0.30) at the cost of ~2.5M parameters and longer training time, while the LSTM + dilated Conv1D model offered a very competitive trade-off between accuracy (~0.29 / 0.32) and computation.

Model comparison: accuracy vs complexity

On an individual RNA, the Transformer closely reproduces the experimental DMS reactivity peaks along the sequence, showing that the model captures both local motifs and longer-range structural patterns.

Real vs predicted DMS reactivity (Transformer, representative RNA)

Code & experiments available on GitHub: github.com/DOS-2024/Prjet-Kaggle_BOUCHAARA_DELIKAYA_DELASSUS_SAM_RNA_Folding

Internship INRAE – 2024

Long-read transcriptomics internship – Iso-Seq PacBio benchmarking
INRAE, Sophia-Antipolis · June 2024 – August 2024

During this internship, I benchmarked long-read transcriptomics tools on Iso-Seq PacBio data and optimised alignment strategies for accurate isoform reconstruction. My work focused on installing, containerising and comparing several pipelines (TAGET, ASAPA, TAMA), and tuning alignment parameters for minimap2 and gmap on an HPC cluster (SLURM, Singularity/Apptainer).

Reducing soft-clipping through alignment parameter tuning [Soft-clipping optimisation]

I evaluated the impact of alignment options (end-bonus, trimming thresholds, splice modes) to minimise soft-clipped regions and improve transcript completeness, using IGV to inspect mapped reads and identify consistent parameter choices across datasets.

Alignment differences on M. incognita reads [GMAP mapping differences]

By comparing GMAP alignments with reference genome annotations (.gff3), I highlighted mismatches between transcript structures and genomic models, useful for downstream isoform interpretation and annotation improvement.

Overall, I contributed by providing a clear benchmarking framework for long-read analysis tools, identifying compatible pipelines and optimising alignment parameters for Iso-Seq datasets. This work improved the reliability of isoform reconstruction and provided the lab with validated HPC-ready workflows for future analyses.

Climate modelling project – 2024

Predicting the population density of Ixodes scapularis over the course of the century
Université Côte d’Azur · BSc Life Sciences (Bioinformatics & Modelling)

In this project I used county-level tick occurrence data from the CDC and regional climate projections (NASA / IPCC SSP2-4.5, SSP1-2.6, SSP3-7.0, SSP5-8.5) to model how temperature will affect the population density of the black-legged tick (Ixodes scapularis) in the United States over the 21st century.

Temperature–density relationship during the active season

I fitted a Gaussian niche model between mean temperature during the active season and tick density. The curve shows an optimum around 20 °C, with a relatively wide tolerance, which we use as the basis for predicting suitability under future warming.

Temperature–density relationship during the inactive season

A similar model for the inactive season shows an optimum around 3.5 °C and a narrower tolerance, revealing how winter conditions constrain the long-term persistence of I. scapularis in colder regions.

Future suitability maps for the United States (2050 & 2100)

For each decade and each SSP scenario, I combined the seasonal temperature projections with the fitted niche models to classify states into five categories of temperature suitability:

  • Unsuitable – red
  • Poorly suitable – orange
  • Acceptable – yellow
  • Favourable – light green
  • Optimal – dark green

The maps for 2050 and 2100 under the four scenarios highlight a transient window of high suitability in many northern states under strong-warming pathways, followed by a decline in suitability by the end of the century, while southern states remain largely unsuitable throughout.

MyBlastp synteny tool – 2023

MyBlastp – interactive synteny visualisation between E. coli strains
Université Côte d’Azur · Programming & Bioinformatics project

In this project, we developed a Python pipeline and graphical interface to analyse protein synteny between different Escherichia coli strains. The tool takes as input the data directory, the protein ID of interest, the Assembly accessions of the query and subject genomes, and the user’s operating system (macOS, Linux or Windows).

Behind the interface, the program parses genome annotation tables, extracts the query protein and its upstream and downstream neighbours, runs BLASTp against the other genomes, and parses the XML output to retrieve the best hits and their genomic positions. These coordinates are then passed to a pygenomeviz-based plotting module to display whether gene order is conserved (“synteny”) or disrupted (“no synteny”) around the protein of interest.

MyBlastp interface and synteny visualisation

The interface is implemented with Tkinter so that biologists can launch complex BLASTp-based synteny analyses without writing any code: one click on Get my synteny! runs the full workflow and displays the resulting figure directly in the application window.
